# Positioning Substitute Risk Research

Status: Focused strategy research draft
Last updated: 2026-06-02

## Purpose

This memo pressure-tests Lingual's emerging positioning:

> A platform where students get engaging, natural English conversation time, while teachers design the pedagogical intent and turn the conversation into classroom evidence.

The question is not whether AI speaking practice exists. It clearly does. The question is whether this exact business position is empty, defensible, or already covered by competitors.

## Executive Verdict

This positioning is **business-viable but not uncontested**.

There are already direct and near-direct competitors converging toward the same destination:

- **Speakology AI** is closest on AI conversation + teacher control + gradebook.
- **Flowent** is closest on "default speaking layer for language programs" + per-session evidence.
- **Speakable** is strongest on K-12 school workflow, evidence, district trust, and assessment.
- **Nualang** is strongest on teacher-created language activities, roleplay/chatbot practice, Wayside distribution, and student engagement.
- **Plang School** is strongest in Korea on school assessment language, education-office signals, and process-assessment evidence.
- **Telo AI** is strongest for ELL speaking-time infrastructure and district procurement signals.
- Newer products such as **Tolkee, BlaBla, b4speak, Beyond Words, LinguaPal, TrySpeak, Gliglish, Eluency, and Bantrly** show the broader market is moving quickly toward AI speaking plus dashboards.

So the answer is:

> No, this is not an empty market. But yes, there is still a meaningful wedge if Lingual owns the complete teacher-controlled conversation-to-instruction loop better than competitors.

The position is weak if Lingual says:

> AI English conversation practice.

The position is stronger if Lingual says:

> Teacher-designed AI conversations that students actually want to finish, with every session becoming debriefs, transcript evidence, class patterns, and next-step teaching decisions.

## Market Reality

The market has moved past novelty. Multiple products can now claim:

- AI speaking practice.
- Natural conversation or roleplay.
- Teacher dashboard.
- Transcripts or recordings.
- Instant feedback.
- Standards or proficiency alignment.
- Assignment or activity creation.
- Progress reports.
- LMS, roster, privacy, or procurement readiness.

This means Lingual cannot win by having one of these features. Lingual must win by combining them into a tighter learning loop than competitors.

## Competitor Coverage

### 1. Speakology AI

Speakology is the closest direct threat in the US and higher-ed world-language market.

Why it overlaps:

- AI-teacher video calls.
- Custom conversation topics.
- ACTFL/WIDA/CEFR/AP/IB alignment language.
- Teacher-created course and conversation modules.
- Instant feedback.
- Gradebook.
- Transcripts and timestamps.
- Oral exam workflow.
- Yale Canvas availability and public institutional signal.

Business read:

Speakology can plausibly cover much of Lingual's proposed position. Its public docs describe natural AI conversations, custom topics, transcripts, grades, and teacher dashboard workflows. Lingual should assume Speakology is already competing for the "teacher-guided AI conversation" buyer.

Where Lingual can still differentiate:

- Less avatar-first, more teacher-workflow-first.
- Stronger assignment generation from real class materials.
- Better class-level pattern detection and next-step recommendations.
- More visible pilot reporting for school administrators.
- Stronger support for colloquial/pragmatic language goals rather than only proficiency/grammar scoring.

### 2. Flowent

Flowent is a very close strategic competitor, especially for language programs, universities, and language schools.

Why it overlaps:

- It explicitly says speaking is the hardest skill to scale.
- It offers scenario-based real-time AI speaking tasks.
- It avoids open chat by aligning tasks to curriculum and communicative goals.
- It gives instructors per-session scores, transcripts, feedback records, cohort patterns, and at-risk visibility.
- It positions itself as the default speaking layer for language programs.
- It uses a paid-pilot expansion motion.

Business read:

Flowent is probably the clearest public articulation of the same strategic destination outside K-12. If Lingual's ambition is "the speaking layer for language programs," Flowent is already using that language. This validates the market but reduces uniqueness.

Where Lingual can still differentiate:

- K-12/high-school readiness instead of university/language-program-first.
- US and Korea school compliance/pilot packaging.
- Teacher-created engaging class activity loop, not only individual scenario practice.
- Stronger student engagement and classroom re-entry after AI practice.

### 3. Speakable

Speakable is the strongest US K-12 institutional benchmark.

Why it overlaps:

- It is built for ELL and world-language programs.
- It combines practice, assessment, portfolios, benchmarks, AI Studio, privacy, LMS integrations, and district claims.
- Public materials mention thousands of school districts, measurable feedback growth, time savings, and K-12 trust posture.
- It now includes simulated conversations, making it more threatening to Lingual's conversation wedge.

Business read:

Speakable may not be the most natural conversation product, but it may be the easiest school buyer substitute because it already looks procurement-ready. If a school wants evidence, rubrics, portfolios, LMS, and district comfort, Speakable is a strong alternative.

Where Lingual can still differentiate:

- More natural adaptive conversation.
- More explicit teacher intent and conversation design.
- Better student engagement during the speaking session.
- Better "what should I teach next?" output instead of grading/portfolio-first workflow.

### 4. Nualang

Nualang is a serious K-12 world-language workflow competitor.

Why it overlaps:

- Teachers can generate/assign exercises, roleplays, and chatbots.
- Students get pronunciation scoring, listening, translation, roleplay, chatbot activities, discussion, and gamified engagement.
- Teachers manage courses, track progress, review pronunciation attempts, and give feedback.
- Wayside's AI-enhanced Nualang chatbot adds real-time feedback while keeping practice inside teacher-selected content and curricular goals.
- Wayside distribution gives it a channel advantage in world-language classrooms.

Business read:

Nualang is not just a chatbot. It is a classroom activity system with publisher/channel leverage. It can cover much of the "teacher controls the flow, students practice, teacher sees evidence" area.

Where Lingual can still differentiate:

- Deeper realtime voice-first multi-turn conversation.
- Stronger debrief and transcript evidence.
- More advanced teacher next-action recommendations.
- Clearer pilot-to-admin report.

### 5. Plang School

Plang is the Korea-specific strategic threat.

Why it overlaps:

- It owns Korean school vocabulary: process-centered assessment, performance assessment, teacher feedback, student evidence, special-activity-record support.
- It covers writing, reading, speaking, and listening.
- It has Seoul and Jeonnam education-office signals.
- Its official pages describe AI + teacher feedback, speech/pronunciation/content feedback, reading scaffolding, listening assessment, and student-record-style activity evidence.

Business read:

Plang is not necessarily the deepest open-ended conversation competitor. But in Korea, buyer trust and assessment language matter more than product category purity. Plang can block Lingual even if Lingual has better conversation quality.

Where Lingual can still differentiate:

- Real multi-turn conversation rather than repeat/record/pronunciation-centered speaking.
- Narrower speaking-evidence wedge instead of broad four-skill assessment.
- International-school, institute, or partner-led entry before public-school scale.
- Clear Korean pilot report showing speaking time, transcript evidence, and teacher actionability.

### 6. Telo AI

Telo is the ELL-specific speaking-time threat.

Why it overlaps:

- ELL students get 1:1 conversational AI through robot or web platform.
- Teachers choose conversation, lesson practice, roleplay, skill training, or ask-and-learn modes.
- It claims teacher dashboard, standards mapping, vocabulary growth, session length, error types, Clever/ClassLink, FERPA, and no training on student data.

Business read:

Telo is very strong if Lingual targets ELL/ML programs. The robot angle is distinctive, but the new web platform makes it less hardware-bound. It can cover much of "more speaking time + teacher insight" for English learners.

Where Lingual can still differentiate:

- Web-first lower-friction language class pilots.
- Teacher-authored assignment flow.
- World-language use cases beyond ELL.
- Better classroom evidence exports.

### 7. Newer / Emerging Direct Competitors

The broader market is converging quickly.

Examples found in this pass:

- **Tolkee**: language schools/universities, teacher-designed scenarios, controlled flow, vocabulary/difficulty/feedback customization, teacher dashboard, pilot motion.
- **BlaBla**: ESL classrooms, turns lesson materials into AI speaking quests, voice chat, transcripts, quest outcomes, coaching insights, student engagement by design.
- **b4speak**: institutional English speaking platform with AI conversation partner/debates, curriculum matching, admin dashboards, bulk onboarding, school reporting.
- **Beyond Words**: universities, schools, training organizations, 4,000+ scenarios, analytics, VR/computer/tablet deployment.
- **LinguaPal / TrySpeak**: white-label education offerings, student management, progress analytics, AI conversation practice, certificates, 30+ language coverage.
- **Gliglish for Education**: unlimited speaking practice, scenarios, reports, exports, many languages, teacher/admin management.
- **Eluency**: teacher-owned content, gamified practice, dashboard, low-friction class code. Less conversation-native but strong on teacher-owned content and engagement.
- **Bantrly**: K-12 speaking confidence, AI voice markers, personalized feedback, teacher dashboards. Public detail is thin but directionally relevant.

Business read:

These products show that the market is not waiting. If Lingual is slow, its positioning may become common category language within 12-24 months.

## Generic AI Substitute Risk

Generic AI tools such as ChatGPT-style voice agents can already cover casual conversation practice very well. They are a real substitute for individual learners and tech-comfortable teachers.

But they are weak substitutes for school adoption because they generally lack:

- Teacher assignment workflow.
- Class roster and student grouping.
- Consent and minor-data controls.
- Transcript/evidence retention policy tuned for schools.
- Gradebook or LMS passback.
- Class-level analytics.
- Standards/proficiency reporting.
- Admin-visible pilot reporting.
- Student-safe guardrails and teacher override.

Generic AI is therefore:

- A **high threat** to consumer AI speaking apps.
- A **medium threat** to teacher-by-teacher informal classroom experiments.
- A **low-medium threat** to serious school pilots where evidence, privacy, and administration matter.

This reinforces the same conclusion: Lingual must not be just a wrapper around AI conversation. It must be the school workflow around conversation.

## Can Existing Competitors Cover Lingual's Position?

Yes, many can cover parts of it.

### Easy to copy

- Basic AI conversation.
- Roleplay scenarios.
- Pronunciation/grammar feedback.
- Student-level transcripts.
- Simple teacher dashboard.
- Rubric scoring.
- Voice/text modes.

### Harder to copy

- Teacher-authored conversation design that feels easy, not technical.
- Natural conversation that stays inside the lesson objective.
- Student engagement loops that make students want to finish.
- Class-level insight that tells the teacher what to do next.
- Pilot reports that administrators can understand.
- Korea-specific process-assessment vocabulary plus local privacy/procurement posture.
- Multi-language support with consistent teacher workflow.
- A product motion that is simultaneously useful to teacher, student, school admin, and later district/government buyer.

The defensible part is not any single feature. The defensible part is the orchestration layer.

## Business Possibility Conditions

Lingual has a real business if these five things become true:

1. **Teachers can create a useful speaking activity in under 5 minutes.**
   If setup is slow, competitors with templates and simpler workflows will win.

2. **Students actually want to finish the conversation.**
   Engagement is not cosmetic. If students treat it like a grading chore, Lingual loses the core product promise.

3. **The conversation produces teacher-usable evidence.**
   A transcript alone is not enough. The teacher needs patterns, target expression usage, rubric evidence, mistakes, strengths, and next-step suggestions.

4. **The pilot report sells the school internally.**
   Early buyers need evidence for principals, department leads, parents, or district contacts. Lingual should package this from the beginning.

5. **Lingual chooses the right first battlefield.**
   US independent/public high-school world language and Korea private/international/institute pilots are more realistic than direct Korea public-school procurement as the first move.

## Strategic Recommendation

The recommended positioning remains valid, but it should be tightened:

> Lingual gives teachers control over AI speaking practice, gives students natural conversations they want to finish, and turns every session into classroom evidence the teacher can act on.

Do not frame Lingual primarily as:

- AI English conversation.
- Pronunciation feedback.
- AI tutor.
- English assessment platform.
- Language-learning app.

Frame it as:

- Teacher-designed speaking practice.
- Natural conversation at student scale.
- Classroom evidence from every conversation.
- Next-step instructional intelligence.
- School-safe pilot reporting.

## What Lingual Should Aim To Prove Next

### Product Proof

- A teacher can turn a lesson objective into a conversation assignment.
- Students complete natural, engaging multi-turn conversations.
- The AI respects the teacher's scenario, target language, difficulty, and success criteria.
- Students get a useful debrief.
- Teachers get class-level patterns and next-step recommendations.
- Admins receive a short pilot report showing usage, speaking time, completion, satisfaction, evidence quality, and implementation friction.

### Market Proof

- 5-10 teachers say this is meaningfully different from assigning generic AI chat.
- 3-5 schools agree to pilot because the reporting/evidence layer matters.
- At least 2 pilot champions say they would pay for continued use or advocate internally.
- Korean educators recognize the language of process assessment and speaking evidence, even if public-school procurement is later.

### Competitive Proof

- In side-by-side demos, Lingual must be better than Speakology/Speakable/Nualang on at least one of these:
  - faster teacher activity creation from real class material;
  - more engaging student conversation;
  - more actionable teacher dashboard;
  - better class/pilot reporting;
  - stronger speaking evidence for assessment conversations.

## Bottom Line

The business is possible, but the bar is higher than "AI speaking practice."

The strongest version of Lingual is:

> The conversation-to-instruction platform for school language programs.

That is commercially meaningful because schools do not only need more speaking. They need speaking practice that teachers can design, students will actually do, and administrators can justify.

The main risk is speed. Competitors are already converging on this language. Lingual should now narrow the wedge, prove the full loop in pilots, and avoid expanding into broad assessment or generic tutoring before the speaking-evidence engine is unmistakably strong.
